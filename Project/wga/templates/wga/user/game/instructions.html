{% elif game_context == 'Example: The Rule' %}

Let's say you wanted to explain the following two scenarios:

  1. Facts: The chicken saw food on the other side of the road.
    Conclusion: The chicken crossed the road.
  2. Facts: The old lady's grandson was on the other side of the street.
    Conclusion: The old lady crossed the street.

Now if you wanted a rule that wanted to explain both of the above, a good candidate would be: IF a bird sees something it wants on the other side of the street, THEN it will cross the street.

This rule certainly explains scenario 1, but it fails to explain scenario 2 because the old lady is (presumably) not a bird. So a better rule is: IF anything sees something it wants on the other side of the street, THEN it will cross the street.

{% elif game_context == 'Example: Update Facts' %}

A player generally updates the facts in the scenarios to make them better support the conclusion. For example, suppose that your rule is "IF a bird sees something it wants on the other side of the street, THEN it will cross the street" and your scenarios say:

  1. Facts: The chicken saw food
  2. Facts: The old lady's grandson was near the food.

You might update the facts as follows to make them better fit the part of the rule that talks about "the street":

  1. Facts: The chicken saw food on the other side of the street.
  2. Facts: The old lady's grandson was on the other side of the street.

Be aware that updating facts must be approved by your opponent! Try phrasing these facts in a way that is uncontroversial (i.e., there should be little to no interpretation needed when reading these facts).

{% elif game_context == 'Example: Add Facts' %}

There must be a fact for each scenario. So if you want to emphasize that some property holds in one scenario, you should specify whether it also holds on the other side. For example, let's say you want to add the fact that chickens have wings, because it's relevant to the conclusion you want to prove. You need to also introduce the analogous fact relating to old women:

  1. Fact: Chickens have wings.
  2. Fact: Old women do not have wings.

Be aware that adding facts must be approved by your opponent! Try phrasing these facts in a way that is uncontroversial (i.e., there should be little to no interpretation needed when reading these facts).

{% elif game_context == 'Example: L1' %}

A critic might attack L.1 if they think that scenario 1 fails to correspond with the IF part of the rule (or vice-versa).

For example, suppose that your rule begins with "IF a bird sees something it wants on the other side of the street" and scenario 1 has these facts:

  • The chicken saw a predator.
  • The chicken has feathers.
  • The chicken sees roadkill on the other side of the street.

Since there is nothing in the facts which suggest that the chicken saw something it wants on the other side of the street, the IF part of the rule is not properly justified and the attack should succeed.
